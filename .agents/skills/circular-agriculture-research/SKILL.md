# Circular Agriculture Research

## Overview

Use this skill to turn broad circular agriculture questions into decision-ready research. Keep the work evidence-first, clearly separate facts from inference, and connect research findings to practical agribusiness and software opportunities.

If the user is trying to decide what to build, use this skill first and end with a ranked set of software-relevant opportunities that can hand off cleanly to the app-builder skill.

## Workflow

1. Frame the research brief before gathering evidence:
   - Define the audience: founder, student, researcher, investor, policymaker, operator, or farmer cooperative.
   - Define the decision: market selection, thesis, software idea, pilot design, literature review, business model, or policy recommendation.
   - Define geography and time window. For this workspace, default to the Philippines and Southeast Asia when the user does not specify.
   - Define included and excluded circular models.
   - If the brief is still vague, ask up to three targeted questions before researching.

2. Use current sources when facts may have changed:
   - Browse for recent market, policy, actor, funding, product, and standards information.
   - Prefer 2020-present sources, unless older foundational studies are important.
   - Cite sources in the response whenever web research is used.
   - Use deep research when the question is broad, comparative, or likely to benefit from an autonomous cited research run.

3. Evaluate each opportunity through five lenses:
   - Scientific evidence: yields, soil health, emissions, nutrient recovery, waste reduction, contamination risk.
   - Unit economics: capex, opex, revenue drivers, margin logic, logistics, input substitution, price sensitivity.
   - Operations: feedstock supply, quality control, processing, seasonality, farmer adoption, distribution.
   - Policy and ecosystem: standards, incentives, grants, local government, universities, NGOs, corporate buyers.
   - Software leverage: data collection, traceability, marketplace matching, logistics optimization, MRV, advisory, financing.

4. Rank the opportunities instead of listing them flat:
   - Identify the strongest near-term opportunity.
   - Identify the best software-enablement opportunity.
   - Identify the highest-risk but potentially high-upside opportunity.
   - State why each one wins or loses in the target geography.

5. Make the output useful for action:
   - Rank opportunities by evidence, feasibility, market pull, and software leverage.
   - Name confidence levels and evidence gaps.
   - Propose first experiments or pilots rather than only describing the topic.
   - If the user asked for a product idea, finish with a concise handoff to the app-builder skill.

## Output Standards

- Do not present sustainability claims as true unless the evidence supports them.
- Label estimates, proxy data, assumptions, and inference.
- Surface contradictions instead of smoothing them away.
- Prefer compact tables for comparison-heavy research.
- End with recommended next steps that can inform a software product, pilot, or research plan.
- Favor decision utility over breadth: a smaller number of well-supported opportunities is better than an exhaustive but shallow map.
