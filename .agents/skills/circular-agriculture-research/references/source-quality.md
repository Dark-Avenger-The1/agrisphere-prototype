# Source Quality Standards

Use these standards to judge evidence and communicate confidence.

## Preferred Source Order

1. Peer-reviewed systematic reviews, meta-analyses, and high-quality field trials.
2. Government, intergovernmental, university, and NGO reports with transparent methods.
3. Company filings, official program data, standards bodies, and credible industry reports.
4. Reputable journalism and expert commentary for market signals, clearly labeled as non-academic evidence.
5. Company marketing, unsourced blogs, and advocacy claims only as leads, not proof.

## Confidence Labels

- High: multiple credible sources agree, methods are transparent, and findings fit the target geography or context.
- Medium: credible evidence exists, but geography, scale, methods, or commercial details are imperfect.
- Low: evidence is early, anecdotal, old, promotional, contradictory, or depends on unverified assumptions.

## Red Flags

- Sustainability benefits with no baseline or metric.
- Market-size claims that cannot be traced to a source or method.
- Academic findings generalized from small lab studies to commercial economics.
- Carbon-credit claims without MRV details, permanence assumptions, or registry context.
- "Circular" claims that ignore transport, contamination, energy inputs, or waste hierarchy.

## Required Distinctions

- Separate scientific evidence from market evidence.
- Separate facts from estimates and inference.
- Separate technical feasibility from business viability.
- Separate policy intent from implemented incentives.
